Background

The rise of centralized AI gateways

We are in an era of exploding LLMs and AI applications. To connect to various models, developers face complex APIs and billing systems.

  • Centralized AI Gateways (e.g. OpenRouter) emerged to solve this.
  • They act as aggregators, providing a unified API to route to different models and settle costs in one place.
  • This model significantly lowers integration costs and improves efficiency.
  • Current Reality: While AI capabilities grow, distribution and access heavily rely on centralized platforms.

Problem

Centralized AI gateways are trust-heavy

Typical routers consolidate pricing, routing, billing, and disputes under one operator. That is convenient, but users cannot easily verify quiet rule changes, whether a charge matched the published policy, or whether records could be rewritten after the fact.

  • Our narrative hook: which parts of the workflow belong on an EVM so rules and settlement are publicly observable and harder to tamper with?
  • We do not claim fully on-chain inference; we trade realism for clearer settlement and auditability.

Solutions

What we move on-chain

  • Provider identity commitments and metadata registered publicly
  • Price updates are not instantly effective; they have observable delay
  • Each paid call leaves an auditable on-chain record
  • Stake can be reduced via an on-chain slashing proposal with a challenge window; proceeds go to configurable slashTreasury
  • External audit outcomes can be anchored as report hashes

Inference stays off-chain for practicality; settlement and auditing become more transparent.

System

Our System contains

  • Smart contract marketplace: registration, stake, delayed price changes, per-call settlement, slashing, audit anchoring
  • Web app: separate user and provider flows; browse, register, invoke (some on-chain detail panels are MVP mock layouts)
  • Relay API: local mock or upstream OpenRouter proxy
  • Audit server: structured checks on the relay; summaries can be written back on-chain

Work Flow

End-to-end journey

  1. Provider registers on-chain: price, stake, metadata URI, identity and capability hashes, endpointCommitment
  2. Web lists providers; users can simulate calls via the relay API
  3. Client hashes request and response, then calls on-chain invoke(...) with payment
  4. Contract applies delayed price when due, transfers fees, refunds overpayment, stores call record, emits CallRecorded
  5. If auditing is enabled: probe relay off-chain → canonical JSON → optional off-chain publish → recordAudit / recordAuditWithUri anchors keccak256(canonicalJson)

Governance

Governance, Roles & Slashing

The Slashing Lifecycle

proposeSlash()

Initiated by slashOperator. Marks the provider as penalized.

challengeSlashProposal()

Provider disputes within the challenge window to block execution.

finalizeSlashProposal()

ETH is seized and routed to the slashTreasury multisig.

Role Rotation Safety

Critical roles (slashOperator, auditRecorder, slashTreasury) require a two-step propose* → accept* handshake to prevent accidental loss of control.

Multi-Auditor Flow

Beyond direct anchoring, a round-based flow (beginAuditRound + attestAudit) allows an allowlisted quorum of auditors to submit findings.

Core Contract Design I

Delayed Price Transparency

Providers publish prices instantly, but enforcement is delayed on-chain.

Key Function announcePriceChange() 
function announcePriceChange(providerId, newPrice) external {
  requireOwner(providerId);
  _applyPendingPrice(providerId, p);
  p.pendingPriceWei = newPrice;
  p.pendingEffectiveBlock = block.number + priceDelayBlocks;
  emit PriceChangeAnnounced(...);
}
Design Intent

Separates announcement from activation. Users verify prices before paying.

Core State

pendingPriceWei and pendingEffectiveBlock enforce a mandatory waiting period in state.

Why It Matters

Turns pricing into an observable rule, eliminating sudden repricing entirely.

Core Contract Design II

Atomic Settlement & Evidence Anchoring

A unified settlement function pays the provider, refunds excess ETH, and anchors evidence atomically.

Settlement

Applies the effective price, transfers the fee, and refunds any overpayment immediately.

Evidence

requestHash and responseHash are recorded, keeping raw payloads private.

Result

Leaves an immutable ledger entry for dispute review, analytics, and audits.

Key Function invoke() 
function invoke(...) external payable {
  _applyPendingPrice(providerId, p);
  uint256 paid = p.pricePerCall;
  if (msg.value < paid) revert PaymentTooLow();

  payProvider(p.owner, paid);
  refundCaller(msg.value - paid);

  calls[nextCallId++] = CallRecord({
    requestHash, responseHash, paid, ...
  });
  emit CallRecorded(...);
}

Limits & Future Work

Current Limits and Next Directions

The MVP proves the settlement model, but stronger guarantees require further infrastructure.

Limitation

Slashing relies on centralized operators and multisigs.

Direction

Introduce decentralized arbitration and trust-minimized governance.

Limitation

Settlement is immediate and single-shot.

Direction

Support escrowed, staged, and streaming payments.

Limitation

Audit quorums and aggregation happen off-chain.

Direction

Move aggregation into an AVS or oracle layer for public verification.

Limitation

No cryptographic proof of honest off-chain inference.

Direction

Integrate TEEs or zk-proofs for verifiable execution.
